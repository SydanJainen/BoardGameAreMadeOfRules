COMPLEXITY: 2.9
PLAYERS: 1-5
DURATION: 40-70

## Detailed Analysis

### Engine Building
Wingspan's central mechanic is engine building. Each habitat row on a player's mat functions as an engine that grows more powerful as birds are added. When a player takes an action in a habitat, they activate all brown-powered birds in that row from right to left, generating cascading benefits. Early-game investments in birds compound over subsequent rounds, producing increasingly larger yields of food, eggs, or cards per single action.

### Card Drafting
Players draft bird cards from a shared display of three face-up cards or the top of the deck. The bird tray acts as a visible market, creating tension between taking a card you need now versus leaving it available for opponents. The tray is refreshed at end of round and replenished during play only after a player's turn ends, so timing matters.

### Set Collection
Multiple scoring vectors reward collecting sets. Bonus cards grant points for accumulating birds with specific traits -- particular nest types, habitat preferences, food requirements, or other attributes. End-of-round goals similarly reward having the most birds or eggs in specific habitats or nest types, driving players to collect matching groups.

### Hand Management
Players begin with a crucial hand management decision: keeping up to 5 birds from an initial draw of 5, but sacrificing 1 food token per bird kept. Throughout the game, there is no hand limit, but birds in hand are dead weight until played. Players must balance drawing cards (wetland action) against having enough food and eggs to actually deploy them. The card-to-food conversion slots offer a pressure valve for excess cards.

### Dice Rolling
The birdfeeder dice tower provides a randomized food market. Five custom dice are rolled into the tower, and players select dice to gain corresponding food tokens. The reroll rule (all dice reset when only one face value remains or the tray is empty) adds a push-your-luck element. Certain predator birds also trigger dice rolls for their activated powers, adding further variability.

### Tableau Building
Each player constructs a personal tableau of up to 15 birds across three habitat rows (5 per row). Bird placement is permanent and sequential (leftmost open slot), so the order of placement within a row affects both the cost structure (egg costs increase in later columns) and the sequence of power activations. The tableau is the primary visible game state for each player and determines their strategic capacity.

### Additional Observations

**Asymmetric Rounds**: The decreasing action count per round (8, 7, 6, 5) as cubes are committed to the goal board creates natural pacing. Early rounds favor building infrastructure; later rounds favor exploiting the engine.

**Dual Goal System**: The green (competitive majority) and blue (individual target) goal board sides offer two distinct competitive dynamics, affecting how aggressively players must monitor opponents.

**Pink Powers**: The "once between turns" reactive powers on certain birds add limited player interaction in an otherwise low-interaction design, giving opponents reasons to pay attention during other players' turns.

**Food Economy**: The five food types with a 2-for-1 wild conversion provide flexibility while still constraining choices. The birdfeeder's dice-based market means food availability is partially stochastic, requiring adaptive planning.

**Complexity Assessment**: At 2.9 on the BGG-style weight scale, Wingspan sits at the upper end of gateway complexity. The rules themselves are straightforward (four actions, three habitats), but the 170 unique bird cards with varied powers, multiple scoring paths, and engine optimization decisions give the game meaningful strategic depth without heavy mechanical overhead.
